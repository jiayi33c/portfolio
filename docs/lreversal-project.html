L-reversal in Genetics

Project Summary

My final year project studies how mathematical models of card shuffling can be used to understand genome rearrangements. I focus on the L reversal Markov chain, where each step randomly inverts a contiguous block of up to L elements, and I analyze its mixing time, meaning the number of reversals needed before the order looks essentially random. Building on results from Markov Chains and Mixing Times, I prove new upper and lower bounds on the mixing time of the L reversal chain, including a comparison with the classical adjacent transposition shuffle using spectral gap estimates. I also extend the model to a weighted version where different block lengths are chosen with different probabilities and show how the mixing time depends on this choice. These results connect probabilistic shuffling theory with models of genome inversions and provide a framework for studying inversion driven genome evolution and its possible medical applications.
